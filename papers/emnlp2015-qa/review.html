Comments

This manuscript presents a study of schema inference for binary relations in open information extraction. The authors propose a system to infer the type distributions of two arguments for binary relation simultaneously. Then, they generate a ranking list of human-readable type pairs for a binary relation. In general the work is important and the approaches make sense, except some confusing expressions and writings.

Here some suggestions to improve the work:

- The authors only report the performance of their own system in this paper. I was surprised why no comparison with the start-of-the-art method or a strong baseline was provided to demonstrate the difficulty of this task and justified the effectiveness of their method. For this reason, it is hard to appraise this work.

- I think one contribution is that this paper proposes a framework to model the semantic representation of binary relations. But I really want to know how it works with the help of the analysis of the experimental results more than only the reports of performance.

- How to determine the parameter settings by "empirics" (in the 4th paragraph of Sec 4)? It may be helpful for the understanding of the authorsâ€™ work and easily reproduce the method for other investigation.

Comments

This paper presents a technique for inferring schemas for Open IE relations. It's an important task and the paper includes a number of interesting techniques, and reasonable experiments against a decent baseline. A handful of areas for improvement exist, regarding both clarity and the methods utilized.

Most of the paper is quite clear, but there are a few places that bring up big questions. First, the task definition in Section 2 seems more restrictive than what is really addressed in the paper. The task talks about outputting a single type pair for a given relation, but actually a ranking of type pairs is what is output and evaluated, and multiple correct answers are possible (this is the right task to evaluate -- it's only the definition in Sec 2 that is too restrictive, not the experiments). Also Section 2 defines the task as always preferring largest coverage, but breaking ties to prefer the most specific types; this should not be given as a property of the task, it's a property of one solution to the task. It's also not really the solution the paper uses, because the paper's method has a softer method that balances specificity and coverage somewhat (equation 6).

Regarding the methods: I liked the matching score derived through Equation 3. The approach is intuitive and well-described. Mentioning explicitly up-front that $n$ comes from the KB and $a$ is from an extracted tuple would be helpful.

Equation 4, on the other hand, is really very heuristic and I would encourage the authors to not call what is computed there a probability. I don't see a reason why it is sound to treat the normalized score (computed from log idf terms) as a probability. Just referring to the product in Eq 4 as a score should be sufficient, given how this quantity is used. Finally, I never did understand what the last factor P(1_or_2_hop_rel) in equation 4 meant, which is unfortunate because it seems central to the approach.

The experiments are generally okay for a short paper, although what would help is an evaluation that goes beyond the subjective schema task itself to one of the end tasks that motivates the work. One option is using schema to de-noise the OpenIE results, which can still be quite low precision. Previous work has used selectional preferences for this task in Open IE, see e.g.: Sparse Information Extraction: Unsupervised Language Models to the Rescue. Downey et al., ACL 2007

Smaller suggestions/questions:

Are the differences in Table 2 statistically significant?

3.2 could be improved by (a) being more formal about the transformation, and (b) including a concrete example

The paper says "we make the following parameter settings by empirics" -- it would be better to say these parameters were tuned using a development set

The paper says "we use selectional preference as our baseline model" -- but I thought "selectional preference" is the name of the task, not a method. Instead, if the pointwise mutual information measure for selectional preferences comes from previous work, that work should be cited here.
