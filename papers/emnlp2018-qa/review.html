Review #1

What is this paper about, and what contributions does it make?

They propose an approach to handle complex questions over knowledge bases by encoding the query graph. They also proposed to use dependency parse trees to enrich the query representations and an ensemble method to enrich an entity linking.

What strengths does this paper have?

The paper is well-organized and well-written. It is easy to follow their approach using the given examples.

What weaknesses does this paper have?

Some details are missing (e.g. how the SPARQL queries are generated), so it might not be easy to replicate the work.

Overall Recommendation:4

Questions for the Author(s)

It would be nice if you can explicitly define what the answer node is.

A brief explanation (or a reference) is needed on how to generate SPARQL queries.

What is the query component and how they are detected?

Review #2

What is this paper about, and what contributions does it make?

The authors propose a semantic parsing method for answering questions that involve multiple entities and relations with a KB. The system uses dependency information and entity linking from several sources, and it matches queries based on pooling representations of several simpler graphs. Results on ComplexQuestions and WebQuestions are very competitive, while the model also maintains strong performance for SimpleQuestions.

What strengths does this paper have?

  • Experiments on 3 different datasets, including one with Simple Questions to check that performance does not suffer for simpler questions

  • well written and well structured

  • good use of examples

  • substantial amount of experiments and ablations

  • individual model choices are tested in isolation in dedicated experiments

  • qualitative error analysis

  • Figure 2 gives a nice overview for the Query Graph Generation

  • Entity linking is identified as performance bottleneck, effective solution proposed

What weaknesses does this paper have?

  • improvements with proposed method are marginal (in terms of F1)
Overall Recommendation:3.5

Questions for the Author(s)

  • In 3.3, it was surprising to see that ignoring entity id information does deteriorate performance, but very little. Is this due to the model being somewhat robust and able to find relevant information from other parts, or is this a problem with the datasets?

  • Table 4: How was the ablation that represented path information as None set up, i.e. what was put into the model?

  • Does the varying number of entities / focus nodes affect the outcome of the pooling? Is it easier or harder for cases where there are fewer graphs?

Presentation Improvements

  • footnotes 4 and 6 reach into the neighbouring column.

  • it would aid the reader to state explicitly what the sum variable i denotes in section "Semantic composition and question representation"

Typos, Grammar, and Style

  • Section 2.1 "mathods"

  • end of Section 3.2: "these two system"

  • end of section 3: "was died in 2006"


Review #3

What is this paper about, and what contributions does it make?

  • This paper considers the problem of semantic parsing of questions in order to answer queries using a Knowledge Base. Specifically, the paper is focused on the setting where complex questions are provided, with multiple entities and relationships that make it difficult to parse the question and handle the complicated query graphs that arise.

Contributions:

  • Once a collection of candidate query graphs (or semantic parse trees, query trees, etc. in the literature) have been made, this paper proposes a new method for encoding the query graphs and corresponding question into the same vector space to allow comparison. This comparison allows for the selection of the best candidate query graph. The method is novel in that is considers the global structure of the complex query graph (rather than simply averaging or concatenating local subgraphs)

  • Though not the main focus, this paper proposes a new method for generated candidate query graphs, using the staged candidate generation method (originally discussed in Bao et al. 2016)

  • Also proposes a simple method to generate additional entity links through a method similar to self-supervision, leveraging the S-MART system.

What strengths does this paper have?

  • Well motivated problem of complex queries and their difficulties. Why it is necessary to employ a semantic similarity matching model that takes global information about the question and graph into account.

  • Solid empirical results, reaching state of the art on ComplexQuestions. Also goes beyond other published methods and evaluates on three datasets, also providing (albeit brief) qualitative analysis of their model's failures

  • Small ablations into the different architecture choices (pooling vs. GRU, dependency structures)

What weaknesses does this paper have?

  • 1.) The main issue I have is that it is not clear where the performance gains are coming from, is it from the generation of the query graphs (an improved version of Bao et al. 2016), or are the gains from the new method for calculating the similarity between questions and graphs. The introduction, abstract, and title of the paper only describe the contribution of this work as the new semantic matching model. The reader is then suprised to see a very complex new method for staged candidate generation.

    • If you indeed have constructed a powerful semantic matching model that gets around failures of past works, why not use the staged candidate generation method of Bao et al. 2016 and show how substituting your semantic matching model can improve the failures of their approach.
    • If the new staged candidate generation method is powerful, then use a simple way to encode the meaning of the query graphs (perhaps the simple method of breaking down into the subgraphs as described in the introduction) and show how it improves over the baseline candidate generating
    • Once the efficacy of the two approaches is established, then combine them and observe how their combination provides further (or worse) improvements

  • 2.) It is not clear which of the (many) pieces of the proposed staged candidate generation method are new, and which consist of pieces proposed in past work. It would be beneficial to describe how past approaches work, i.e. Bao et al. 2016, Luo et al. 2015, and how your proposed methods differ from past work. On a related note, the selection of these five steps to generating query graphs seem a bit arbitrary, without any motivation in the introduction or in Section 2. If you can identify how past approaches performed candidate generation, describe their failures and where your steps correct them, that would be useful for understanding your method.

  • The quality of writing and description of the method and overall paper can be improved.

  • Difficult to replicate the entire model as there are many moving pieces. Would be useful for authors to release code, or provide more in-depth analysis of implementation details in an Appendix.

Overall Recommendation:4

Questions for the Author(s)

  • The proposed approach is described as light-weighted, as an advantage of the method. What does this mean? It has few parameters to learn? It is easy to implement?

Missing References

  • Around line 114, is there a citation here? Who has tried to do this approach where you say it is intuitive? Do you beat this baseline?

  • The related work section adds very little to the paper as it is very high level, not comparing/contrasting individual decisions of your approach. Perhaps the relevant pieces of this section can be integrated into the description of your method to make your method clearer.

Presentation Improvements

  • The title, abstract, introduction, and conclusion make no mention of the new candidate query generation system. The paper should highlight this method, and explain how it is similar and different to past works. Especially considering that the description of your candidate query generation method is very hard to follow.

  • It would be useful in Table 2 to group the related approaches into categories rather than by year. For example "semantic parsing approaches" vs. "memory networks", and perhaps a small asterisk if the model "uses external data".

Typos, Grammar, and Style

  • There is no motivation for why KBQA and semantic parsing is an interesting research topic in the paper (We both agree that it is). The only motivation given (besides describing what the problem is) is that it is a "hot" research area in the related work. Should include a short snippet about why the reader should care about your paper.

  • Is the semantic matching model agnostic to the method of constructing the query graphs, either bottom-up parsing or staged query generation? If so, would be nice to mention your approaches flexibility.

Minor Edits:

  • 181 "mathods"

  • 409 "we"

  • 412 "similarity"

  • Footnote 4 overruns Errors like these are abundant through the paper, please use a grammar/spell checker before publication.
