Summary of the paper (what is being proposed and in what context) and a brief justification of your overall recommendation. One paragraph This is a paper on natural language processing, and it considers a
special case of the so called Selectional Preferences problem:
associate with every verb a characterization of the entities typically
used as its subjects and objects, as observed in a given text
collection. Here, the taxonomy is taken from Probase. The paper
identifies some goodness properties of desired collections of
characterizing concepts, such as using concepts that are general on
the one hand, yet having small overlap on the other hand. Then, the
paper translates this problem into a formal computational problem,
shows a complexity lower bound, and devises heuristic
solutions. Several experiments are provided, where the authors make a
comparison among the heuristics, and illustrate the quality obtained
by using their solution in various tasks, compared to
alternatives. The paper has interesting ideas, but it does not well
define the problem at hand beyond examples and informal wording, and
the experimental study is not sufficiently clear. Most importantly,
the paper does not well reference the literature on selectional
preferences.
Three (or more) strong points about the paper (Please be precise and explicit; clearly explain the value and nature of the contribution). - The paper attacks an important, interesting, and challenging problem.

- The paper seems to present novel and valid techniques.

- The experimental study is rich.

- The presentation is substandard.

Detailed Evaluation (Contribution, Pros/Cons, Errors); please number each point As said above in the summary, I believe that this paper presents
interesting ideas. The reported research should be pursued and
complemented with a more thorough investigation of related art, and a
better exposition. Below, I detail my reservations on this paper.

I will first discuss the introduction. In my opinion, the
introduction does not do justice to the content of the paper. The main
message I got is that FrameNet is too coarse grained, ReVerb and
TextRunner are too fine grained, so we want something in between. But
for every classification you can find tasks where it is likewise
overly general or overly specific. The introduction does not give any
clear definition of what it is the paper is trying to achieve, but is
rather based on examples and informal wording (notable is the
excessive use of italic font to express informality, without and
formal backing). Moreover, the introduction makes statements that
require further explanation. For example, what is the problem with
SRL's labels being unintuitive? Is it just about the specific
terminology used by the designers, which can be easily overridden?
The idea that SRL lexicons cannot "get involved in automatic
computation such as similarity or summarization" is ignoring the work
on automatic identification of roles, such as:

L. Shi, R. Mihalcea: Putting Pieces Together: Combining FrameNet,
VerbNet and WordNet for Robust Semantic Parsing. 100-111

Similarly, the statement that ReVerb "cannot be used in computation
directly" ignores the plethora of possible problems one may need to
solve. Also, one can argue that a type like “color” is too fine
grained, and what is really needed is "fabric features." I am not
claiming that "color" is bad, but rather that goodness is relative to
the application and users, and that it is not very helpful to label
goodness here as a general property. I believe that a better story
for the introduction would emphasize the need to classify the verb
arguments while being able to control the level of granularity (rather
than subscribing to existing lexicons), and to do so automatically.

Selectional Preference, which has been well studied in the NLP
community, and is cited in this paper, seems to generalize the problem
studied in here. For example, this is the definition of Ritter et al:
"Selectional Preferences encode the set of admissible argument values
for a relation." Here, the relations discuss are between a verb and
its subjects, and between a verb and its objects.

Alan Ritter, Mausam, Oren Etzioni: A Latent Dirichlet Allocation
Method for Selectional Preferences. ACL 2010: 424-434

The authors seem to dismiss the literature on this topic based on the
statement that "While our problem determines the appropriate
abstraction of instances of verb argument, selectional preference
doesn’t necessarily output a representation for plausible arguments."
It seems to be inconsistent with Ritter et al. In fact, the authors
use "selectional preference" as a baseline in their experiments, but
do not mention which algorithm for selectional preference is actually
being used. The authors should have given a much deeper reference and
comparison to that line of work.

The formal computational problem that the authors phrase could be
defined as a quadratic integer program (by multiplying the LHS of (4)
with xs and xt). Why has not such a mathematical solver been used,
instead of the authors' proposed heuristics? The authors should
discuss this point.

Space is not being best used in this paper. The box of Algorithm 4 is
redundant. All it does is to count the number of occurrences of a
member of each concept. This could be defined with a simple
sentence. On the other hand, nothing is said about the manner in which
the neighborhood is being defined for simulated annealing, which is
the core of this technique. Instead, the reader is being referred to a
40 line pseudocode without comments. In addition, some experiments
deserve more explanation. For example, what exactly does the dataset
of "Word Similarity 353" provide? How does it translate into an
experiment? In Section 3.3, the authors present several formulas to
define the score of a solution, but no explanation or motivation is
given to those. Do these formulas represent any intuitive quantity?

The use of the term "argument" throughout the paper is confusing. The
standard definition includes indirect objects, but his paper ignores
those. In Section 3 it is said that "the term “argument” refers to
either the object or the subject of a verb," but in Section 4.2 it is
said that "we abuse using the term argument to refer to object of the
verbs in the following experiments." (Why only objects, btw?) Then
again, Section 4 later discusses subjects frequently. Please use a
clear terminology that will avoid the confusion.

The experiment in section 4.4 does not make a clear case. It defines a
process with quite a few apparently ad-hoc decisions (alpha=0.25, the
definition of Co, filters, clusters=130), and reports numbers. Why
should the reader infer that these numbers are relevant beyond the
specific verbs, text, and the specific tuning that took place? I am
missing an evidence of generalization.

The results in the experiment of Section 4.5 seem to be in significant
disagreement with [23]. While the authors claim to use the same
dataset and metrics, the number reported on the performance of [23] is
0.27, with the combined one giving 0.35; but the numbers in [23] range
from 0.67 to 0.92. The authors should explain this gap.

Additional comments:

- Regarding the human judges, please specify how disagreement has been
resolved.

- I couldn't find any reference to the crossed-out words in Figure 2.

- Section 3.4: "I think ranking has the following purposes"

- Many typos and grammar errors throughout the paper.

Summary of the paper (what is being proposed and in what context) and a brief justification of your overall recommendation. One paragraph The paper presents a novel approach to abstract a verb and its arguments into (what the author’s call) action concepts. The author’s define the problem of finding a lexicon of action concepts as a combinatorial optimization problem and provide both a greedy as well as a local search solution. The algorithms themselves seem rather straightforward, so the real contribution is in the problem formalization itself. Given that the paper seems more appropriate for a computational linguistics conference.
Three (or more) strong points about the paper (Please be precise and explicit; clearly explain the value and nature of the contribution). 1. The problem of identifying action concepts seems novel and useful.

2. Experimental results show that the approach works in practice.

The paper seems to be a better fit for a computational linguistics conference. All prior approaches cited in the paper have been published in computational linguistics conferences such as ACL. The main contribution seems to be the ability to generate lexicons of action concepts which seems to have more direct implications for computational linguists than for data management.

2. The experimental evaluation is missing proper comparison with state of the art. Why was [33] chosen for a comparison partner and not one of the more recent approaches discussed in related work?

3. The experimental evaluation focusses exclusively on the 200 most common verbs in English text. To what extent does the approach work for less frequent verbs? What are its limitations?

4. For a VLDB paper people generally expect to see an evaluation of the runtime and scalability of the proposed algorithms. How do they compare to prior approaches? How much does runtime matter, given that this is probably a one-time activity?
